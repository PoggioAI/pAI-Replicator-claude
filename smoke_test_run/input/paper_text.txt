Edge of Stochastic Stability:
Revisiting the Edge of Stability for SGD
Anonymous Authors1
Abstract
Recent findings by Cohen et al. (2021) demon-
strate that when training neural networks with full-
batch gradient descent with step size η, the largest
eigenvalue λmax of the full-batch Hessian consis-
tently stabilizes around 2/η. These results have
significant implications for convergence and gen-
eralization. This, however, is not the case of mini-
batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD), limiting
the broader applicability of its consequences. We
show that SGD trains in a different regime we
term Edge of Stochastic Stability (EOSS). In this
regime, what stabilizes at 2/η is Batch Sharpness:
the expected directional curvature of mini-batch
Hessians along their corresponding stochastic gra-
dients. As a consequence, λmax—which is gener-
ally smaller than Batch Sharpness—is suppressed,
aligning with the long-standing empirical obser-
vation that smaller batches and larger step sizes
favor flatter minima. We further discuss implica-
tions for mathematical modeling of SGD trajecto-
ries.
1. Introduction
The choice of training algorithm is a key ingredient in the
deep learning recipe. Extensive evidence, e.g. Keskar et al.
(2016), indeed shows that performance consistently depends
on the optimizer and hyperparameters. What machinery
induces this optimizer-dependence is a central question in
the theory of deep learning.
Cohen et al. (2021; 2024) answered this question for full-
batch Gradient Descent (GD): it trains neural networks in a
regime of instability—termed the Edge of Stability (EOS)—
following the Central Flow. With a constant step size η, the
highest eigenvalue of the Hessian of the full-batch loss—
denoted here as λmax—increases towards 2/η and hovers

Granular contributions.
We group our contributions into
four themes:
• Conceptual/diagnostic. We define EOSS via saturation
of a sufficient instability criterion and give a checkpoint-
level perturbation test (restart + η ↑or b ↓) that reveals
edge behavior through catapults; oscillations alone are
not an instability signal (GNI certifies oscillations but is
not an instability criterion).
• Theory. We introduce Batch Sharpness and prove on the
local quadratic model that BatchSharpness > (2+ε)/η
yields a one-sided instability certificate via multiplicative
growth of mini-batch gradient norms, identifying (ex-
pected) mini-batch gradient norms as the relevant local
Lyapunov viewpoint.
• Empirics. Across architectures and (η, b), Batch Sharp-
ness progressively sharpens then hovers near 2/η; this
explains batch-size-dependent suppression of the full-
batch λmax and the occurrence of intermittent catapults.
• Implications. EOSS lies outside regimes where uniform-
smoothness descent arguments are informative and high-
lights limits of “GD+noise” / SDE proxies that do not
preserve batch-dependent curvature.
Organization.
Section 2 reviews related work and sharp-
ens the open questions around SGD stability. Section 3
introduces our instability-criterion framework and the
perturbation-based edge diagnostic. Section 4 shows why
oscillations are not a reliable instability signal in SGD. Sec-
tion 6 empirically establishes EOSS and documents catapult
behavior. Section 7 provides our theoretical justification for
Batch Sharpness as a sufficient instability criterion. Finally,
Section 8 discusses implications for curvature/flatness and
for diffusion-style modeling of SGD.
2. Related Work
Progressive sharpening and EOS.
Jastrz˛ebski et al.
(2019; 2020) and Cohen et al. (2021) precisely character-
ized the observation that the local shape of the loss land-
scape changes rapidly at the beginning of the training. They
showed a steady rise in λmax along GD and SGD trajecto-
ries, typically following a brief initial decline. Jastrz˛ebski
et al. (2020) noted the presence of a sharp phase transition,
marking the end of progressive sharpening. Unlike progres-
sive sharpening, this phenomenon is considered to result
from algorithmic instability rather than inherent landscape
properties. Indeed, Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2019; 2020); Cohen
et al. (2021; 2022) demonstrated that this phase transition
comes at different points for different algorithms on the
same landscapes. Cohen et al. (2021; 2022) later showed
that it comes at the instability thresholds, in the case of full-
batch optimization algorithms. E.g., for GD λmax stabilizes
and oscillates around 2/η, for full-batch Adam with stan-
dard hyperparameters λmax of the preconditioned Hessian
does around 38/η. Observations from Cohen et al. (2021;
2022) indicate that, under mean square error (MSE), the
bulk of training dynamics occur within this regime, effec-
tively determining λmax of the final solution. ?Lee & Jang
(2023) explained why in this regime λmax often slightly
exceeds 2/η: the gradients are non-linear, this shifts the
required value depending on higher-order derivatives. A
growing body of research analyzed the surprising mecha-
nism underlying EOS dynamics observed during training
with GD. Damian et al. (2023) propose an explanation under
some, empirically tested, assumptions of alignment of third
derivatives and gradients: the third derivatives functions as
global stabilizer when the system is locally diverging.
Existing EOS work is limited to full-batch methods.
While the empirical behavior of EOS for full-batch algo-
rithms is relatively well-understood, neural networks are
predominantly trained using mini-batch methods. As ex-
plicitly noted by Cohen et al. (2021, Section 6, Appendices
G and H), their observations and analysis do not directly
apply to mini-batch training, and EOS in SGD “does not
center around the (full-batch) sharpness.” Moreover, note
that both the self-stabilization argument and our understand-
ing of progressive sharpening are limited in the mini-batch
regime: (Damian et al., 2023) depends on having "one land-
scape with one curvature", and it is not easily generalizable
to the mini-batch setting; we do not know how progressive
sharpening affects the higher moments of the minibatch Hes-
sian’s distribution. Importantly, our results imply that SGD
is stable if on average the step is stable on the mini-batch
landscape—not on the full-batch landscape, see Section 8.

What was empirically known for SGD.
In the context
of mini-batch algorithms, (i) Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2019; 2020)
noticed that for SGD the phase transition happens earlier
for smaller η or smaller batch size b, but they did not quan-
tify when. (ii) Cohen et al. (2021); Gilmer et al. (2021)
established that initialization and architecture choices affect
stability of SGD, without providing a definitive condition.
(iii) When λmax stabilizes, that always happens at a level
they could not quantify which is below the 2/η threshold
(Cohen et al., 2021; Keskar et al., 2016), see Figure 2, often
without a proper progressive sharpening phase. This leaves
the fundamental questions open:
• In what way the location of convergence of SGD accli-
mates to the choice of hyperparameters?
• What are the key quantities involved?
• Is this motivated by a mechanism similar to progressive
sharpening?
To be more specific, can we characterize the training phe-
nomena in (i), (ii), (iii) above? What determines them?
Does SGD train in an unstable regime?

3. A Framework for Edge of Instability
In this section we set up a minimal formal framework for
how we reason about (in)stability in the rest of the paper.
Later, we characterize an intervention mechanism to un-
derstand when training happens in a regime akin to EOSS.
More detailed discussion in Appendix C.
Certifying Edge of Instability.
Classical Lyapunov-style
conditions (certificates of stability) characterize regions of
hyperparameters for which the iterates are non-expansive; in
the quadratic case these regions can even be made necessary
and sufficient, as in Equation (6). In this work we take
the complementary viewpoint and focus on certificates of
instability. Our instability test is formulated as a purely
sufficient condition for divergence: it is deliberately one-
sided and may be conservative. Our goal is not to describe
the full stability region, but to obtain a tractable scalar test
whose violation yields a robust certificate of instability.
Definition 3.1 (Instability criterion). Consider a training
algorithm (a discrete-time dynamical system) (θt)t≥0 on
a parameter space Θ ⊆Rd with fixed hyperparameters h
(e.g. learning rate, batch size). Let U ⊆Θ be an open set
(typically, a region where a given local approximation of
the loss is trusted). Let f : U →R and c ∈R. We say
that f is a valid instability criterion with threshold c for the
algorithm on U if the following holds:
f(θ0) > c =⇒(θt)t≥0 leaves every compact subset of U.
That is, for any compact K ⊂U containing θ0, there exists
a finite time T such that E[θT |θ0] /∈K. We say that the
instability criterion f is saturated at θ if f(θ) is (approxi-
mately) equal to c; in practice, up to an O(η · poly(log(η)))
tolerance.

Edge of Stochastic Stability
In words: an instability criterion is a scalar quantity f to-
gether with a threshold c such that crossing f > c is suffi-
cient to force divergence from the region U where we trust
a given local model. For a specific f we generally want
the smallest such c, which depends both on f and on the
underlying dynamical system (algorithm, data, loss, archi-
tecture). A canonical example is GD on a quadratic, where
U = Rd, f(θ) = λmax(∇2L(θ)) and c = 2/η: crossing
λmax > 2/η makes GD linearly unstable on any compact
set.
The question of whether an optimizer operates at the Edge
of (Stochastic) Stability thus becomes:
Are there locally-valid criteria of instability that
empirically saturate during training?
Note that for some deterministic linear systems, the dy-
namics either monotonically diverges (GD on quadratics:
λmax > 2/η) or monotonically converges (λmax < 2/η).
Lemma 3.2. λmax is a valid instability criterion for GD on
the quadratic approximation of the objective.
Proof. Just observing that step of GD on a quadratic has the
iterate θt+1 = (I −ηH)θt.
This clean dichotomy is special and can be misleading. A
certificate of instability is generally not simply the converse
of a certificate of stability. For non-linear or stochastic
dynamical systems, there is no tight boundary separating
instability from monotone convergence.
4. Oscillations do not Signal Instability
In the case of full-batch algorithms, EOS manifests through
the emergence of an oscillatory regime. Practically speak-
ing, one recognizes that full-batch GD trains at EOS when
the loss starts oscillating. SGD with fixed step size on con-
vex functions eventually converge to a stationary distribution
(oscillatory behavior). We show that those oscillations are
stable, there exists a local certificate of oscillation—which
we call GNI, which is not an instability criterion.
In particular, oscillations are not the mechanism to
signal EOS-like regimes for mini-batch optimizers.

5. A Mechanism to Test the Edge of Instability
Cohen et al. (2021) proposes a mechanism to show whether
the landscape shows progressive sharpening happens and
whether GD trains at EOS: reducing the step size. This may
seem trivial but it is key. It is the only way to show that
the trajectory is limited by the step size. The curvature will
restart going up, which means that the "self-stabilization"
(Damian et al., 2023) mechanism was active, and the loss
will exit the oscillatory behavior (which signal instability).
What are good mechanisms in this setting?
Importantly, λmax is easy to compute for small models of
size d and datasets of size n. at least for SGD, instability cri-
teria depend on the distribution of the Hessians {HB}B∼Pb.
Most statistics of this distribution can not be computed in
practical scenario, indeed d and n are generally too large to
meaningfully compute anything that needs more that n × d
computations. Most of the cumulants of the distribution
above, and most of such instability criteria require n × dk
with k > 2.
We thus require to develop:
1) a mechanism to understand whether training happens
in a regime akin to EoS (whether there exists at least an
instability criterion saturated).
2) In case, a method to find a computable one.
The first observation is that in the stochastic setting the
n dependence could be eased in some cases in which the
quantity is an expectation over mini-batches, one could
Montecarlo estimate it (which is what we do). Second, we
develop a mechanism below.
A perturbation test for the edge.
Our operational sig-
nature of an edge regime is sensitivity to small destabiliz-
ing hyperparameter perturbations. Concretely, we take
a checkpoint θt from a baseline run with hyperparame-
ters h0 = (η, b, . . .) that appears stable, and we restart
training from θt under a slightly more aggressive setting
h = h0+∆h (e.g. η ↑or b ↓), keeping everything else fixed.
If the perturbed run exhibits a rapid transient runaway—
a large parameter excursion and an associated sharp loss
increase (a “catapult”)—while the baseline run from the
same checkpoint does not, we interpret this as evidence
that training at h0 is operating near an instability boundary.
Stabilizing perturbations (e.g. η ↓or b ↑) should conversely
suppress such events.
Why this works (informal justification).
On the local
quadratic approximation around θt, many sufficient instabil-
ity certificates take the form f(θt; h) > c(h) ⇒divergence
from the trusted neighborhood. If a candidate criterion
is valid for the local model and monotone in a desta-
bilizing direction of h, then saturation at the baseline
(f(θt; h0) ≈c(h0)) implies that an arbitrarily small desta-
bilizing perturbation yields f(θt; h) > c(h), which in turn
forces divergence of the quadratic-model dynamics. This
divergence manifests in practice as a catapult / loss spike
when running the perturbed optimizer from the same check-
point. Conversely, if a quantity appears to “saturate” but
small destabilizing perturbations do not trigger catapults,
then that quantity is not a valid instability criterion for the
relevant local dynamics. This turns “training at the edge”
into a falsifiable, checkpoint-level diagnostic.
Mechanism (Edge Test): Restart from a checkpoint θt
and apply a small destabilizing perturbation (e.g. η ↑).
Edge signal: perturbed run catapults (large excursion +
loss spike) while baseline does not; stabilizing
perturbations suppress it.
See Appendix C for more details and proof.
6. SGD Typically Occurs at the EOSS
We characterize here the phenomenon of the Edge of
Stochastic Stability. We verify the emergence of EOSS
across of a range of step sizes, batch sizes and architectures
(Figure 3 and Appendix T); datasets (CIFAR-10 and SVHN,
Appendix U).
(1) Edge of Instability and Batch Sharpness stabilizing.
SGD typically trains in a regime we call Edge of Stochastic
Stability. Precisely, after a phase of progressive sharpen-
ing, Batch Sharpness (which we prove in Section 7 to be a
valid local instability criterion) reaches a stability level of
2/η, and hovers there. See Appendix E for the intervention
experiments. In particular, the level of plateau of Batch
Sharpness is 2/η independent of the batch size (Figure 1).
Analogously to EOS, training continues and the loss contin-
ues to decrease while Batch Sharpness is constrained by the
step size magnitude.
(2) Stabilization of λmax and GNI.
Crucially, oscillations
happen throughout most of the training as highlighted by
GNI stabilizing at 2/η very early, see Appendix D, but
they do not impact progressive sharpening which leads to
the second phase of EOSS stabilization which is EOS-type
regime. Also λmax stabilizes at EOSS, but it consistently
settles at a lower level, due to a batch-size–dependent gap

sampled batches exhibits anomalously high sharpness—that
is too high for the stable regime—and steps overshoot, trig-
gering a catapult (Figure 4). This causes a spike in the loss
(Appendix C.3), after which the trajectory either diverges or
re-enters a stable region. If in this region Batch Sharpness
is strictly less than 2/η, this leads to a renewed phase of
progressive sharpening, eventually returning to the EOSS
regime. This aligns with, and maybe explains, previous
observations about catapult behaviors, e.g., (Lewkowycz
et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2024).
7. On Batch Sharpness
Batch Sharpness was introduced in Definition 1.1 as the ex-
pectation of the Rayleigh quotient between the mini-batch
gradient ∇LB (the step direction) and the mini-batch Hes-
sian H(LB). It therefore measures the expected directional
curvature of the mini-batch loss surface along the direc-
tion of the step. We will discuss Batch Sharpness and its
meanings in this Section.

Lyapunov function is the squared norm of the mini-batch
gradients,
V (θt) := ∥∇LB(θt)∥2.

8. Implications
Appendix B contains a discussion and additional context.
• Descent-lemma proof templates fail at EOSS.
Neural-network SGD typically trains in a regime
where step-by-step monotone descent arguments
based on uniform smoothness/stability do not apply.
• What “large step size” means for SGD. The
relevant finite-η instability comparator is Batch
Sharpness (not the full-batch λmax), and empirically
it hovers near the natural threshold 2/η.
• Stabilization becomes distributional. Under
progressive sharpening, “where SGD stabilizes” can
depend on the full distribution of mini-batch Hessians
{HB}B, not only on H.
• “Flatness” could be stability-grounded: what SGD
must control in EOSS is Batch Sharpness hovering
near 2/η, so links between generalization and
curvature proxies (trace/Fisher/λmax, etc.) may have
to factor through how those proxies relate to Batch
Sharpness along the trajectory.
• Limits of “GD + noise” and diffusion/SDE
surrogates. SDE modeling of SGD is insufficient
under progressive sharpening, standard diffusion
limits wash out discrete-time step-size stability
thresholds (η →0).
Failure of classical optimization arguments.
Classical
analyses of GD/SGD certify progress by enforcing uniform
smoothness/stability conditions along the trajectory (e.g. for
a local quadratic model, η ≤2/λmax), yielding a descent
lemma and step-by-step monotonic decrease of a Lyapunov
function. The EOS phenomenon makes the breakdown of
this template unavoidable: full-batch GD routinely oper-
ates at (or beyond) the textbook boundary, the loss is non-
monotone, and yet training progresses; this is not merely a
“nonconvexity issue” and can occur even in convex settings
where trajectories need not converge to stationary points in
the usual sense (Ahn et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). For
SGD, the usual defensive move—assuming a worst-case
smoothness bound over all mini-batch objectives—is typi-
cally vacuous in modern networks. Our framework instead
identifies the operational, direction-aware curvature statistic

Failure of arguments about minima.
This same argu-

Much of the implicit-bias/minima-selection literature as-

on a fixed landscape and converge to (approximate) station-

better described as stabilization in a region that need not
contain stationary points (Ahn et al., 2022; Damian et al.,
2023), and stability is controlled by the mini-batch geom-
etry SGD actually experiences. Consequently, “location”
becomes inherently distributional: it can depend on the full
distribution of mini-batch Hessians (including higher-order
statistics), not just the mean Hessian of the full-batch loss,
and any link between generalization and curvature proxies
(trace/Fisher/λmax, etc.) must factor through how those
proxies relate to Batch Sharpness along the trajectory.
Implication on SGD modeling.
Finally, this distribu-
tional viewpoint clarifies the limits of “SGD ≈GD + noise”
and standard diffusion/SDE modeling: perturbing gradi-
ents while failing to preserve the relevant batch-dependent
curvature statistics defines a different dynamical system,
and it can stabilize in qualitatively different regions. Em-
pirically, only perturbations that preserve the mini-batch
sampling structure reproduce EOSS-like behavior (e.g. sup-
pressed/plateauing λmax), whereas diagonal/isotropic noise
injections and standard SDE integration can fail (Fig. 6;
Appendix J). This suggests that faithful continuous-time
models for EOSS likely require explicitly modeling batch-
dependent curvature (eigenvalues and eigenspaces) beyond
mean-field or covariance-only descriptions.
9. Conclusions, Limitations, and Future Work
Conclusions.
We have addressed the longstanding ques-
tion of if and how mini-batch SGD enters a regime remi-
niscent of the “Edge of Stability” previously observed in
full-batch methods. Contrary to the usual focus on the global
Hessian’s top eigenvalue, we uncovered that Batch Sharp-
ness—the expected directional curvature of the mini-batch
landscape in the direction of its own gradient—consistently
rises (progressive sharpening) and then hovers around 2/η,
independent of batch size. This behavior characterizes a
new regime “Edge of Stochastic Stability”, which explains
how mini-batch training can exhibit catapult-like surges and
settle into flatter minima even when the full-batch Hessian
remains below 2/η. Our analysis clarifies why smaller batch
sizes and larger step sizes both constrain the final curvature
to a lower level, thereby linking these hyperparameters to
flatter solutions and often improved generalization. Further-
more, we show that this phenomenon depends on the noise
injected into the Hessians by mini-batch optimizers, high-
lighting important limitations of SDE-based approximations.
Overall, the EOSS framework unifies several empirically
observed effects—catapult phases, dependence on batch
size, and progressive sharpening—under a single perspec-
tive focused on the mini-batch landscape and its directional
curvature.
Limitations.
(i) We have tested only image-classification
tasks, leaving open whether similar phenomena arise in NLP,
RL, or other domains. (ii) Our experiments mainly use fixed
step sizes and standard architectures, so very large-scale
or large-batch settings remain less explored. (iii) We have
not analyzed momentum-based or adaptive methods (e.g.
Adam), even though full-batch EOS has been seen there
(Cohen et al., 2022).
Future Work.
Beyond addressing these limitations, sev-
eral directions remain: Understanding (i) where λmax sta-
bilizes; (ii) how EOSS and EOS affect performances and
properties of the neural network, e.g. (Lyu et al., 2023;
Arora et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Wang
et al., 2022; Beneventano & Woodworth, 2025); (iii) con-
sequently if it is benign effect or not; (iv) what the other
sources of instability are there in the (pre-)training; (v) bet-
ter describing the phenomenon of progressive sharpening;
and (vi) understanding its causes.

A. Further Related Work
Progressive sharpening.
Early studies observed that the local shape of the loss landscape changes rapidly at the beginning
of the training (Keskar et al., 2016; Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2019; LeCun et al., 2012; Achille et al., 2017; Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2018;
Fort & Ganguli, 2019). Multiple papers noticed growth of different estimators of λmax in the early training (Keskar et al.,
2016; Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2019; Sagun et al., 2016; Fort & Scherlis, 2019). Subsequently, Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2019; 2020)
and Cohen et al. (2021) precisely characterized this behavior, demonstrating a steady rise in λmax along GD and SGD
trajectories, typically following a brief initial decline. This phenomenon was termed progressive sharpening by (Cohen
et al., 2021). To the knowledge of the authors there are no widely accepted explanations of why and in what precise form
progressive sharpening happens, although recent work by Li et al. (2022b); Agarwala et al. (2023) tackle this issue.
A phase transition.
Early works (Goodfellow et al., 2016; Li et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2019; Lewkowycz et al., 2020)
revealed that large initial step sizes often enhance generalization at the cost of initial loss reduction. Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2020)
attributed this to a sudden phase transition, termed the break-even point, marking the end of progressive sharpening. This
transition slows the convergence by confining the dynamics to a region of lower sharpness. Unlike progressive sharpening,
this phenomenon is considered to be induced by the gradient-based algorithm becoming unstable, not by the landscape.
Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2019; 2020); Cohen et al. (2021; 2022) indeed showed that the phase transition comes at different points
for different algorithms on the same landscapes. Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2019; 2020) showed that in the case of mini-batch SGD
this phase comes earlier for bigger step sizes and smaller batch sizes, without quantifying it. Cohen et al. (2021; 2022) later
showed indeed that it comes at the instability thresholds for the full-batch optimization algorithms. We manage here to
quantify the value of the instability threshold for mini-batch SGD, thus characterizing when the phase transition happens for
SGD.
Full-batch edge of stability.
After the phase transition above, GD and full-batch Adam train in the EOS oscillatory
regime (Cohen et al., 2021; 2022; Xing et al., 2018), where the λmax stabilizes and oscillates around a characteristic value.
The name is due to the fact that, in the case of full-batch GD, the λmax hovers at 2/η which is the stability threshold for
optimizing quadratics. Observations from Cohen et al. (2021; 2022) indicate that, under mean square error (MSE), most
training dynamics occur within this regime, effectively determining λmax of the final solution. ?Lee & Jang (2023) explained
why in this regime λmax often slightly exceeds 2/η: the gradients are non-linear, this shifts the required value depending
on higher-order derivatives. A growing body of research analyzed the surprising mechanism underlying EOS dynamics
observed during training with GD. Damian et al. (2023) proposed the "self-stabilization mechanism": an explanation under
some empirically tested assumptions of alignment of third derivatives and gradients: the third derivatives functions as global
stabilizer when the system is locally diverging. Recent work investigated the convergence and implicit regularization aspects
of EOS regime, e.g., see (Arora et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2022; 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2023;
Beneventano & Woodworth, 2025; ?).
The literature was far from EOSS.
While the empirical behavior of EOS for full-batch algorithms is relatively well-
understood, neural networks are predominantly trained using mini-batch methods. As explicitly noted by Cohen et al. (2021,
Section 6, Appendices G and H), their observations and analysis do not directly apply to mini-batch training. In particular,
they emphasize:
[...] while the sharpness does not flatline at any value during SGD (as it does during gradient descent), the trajectory
of the sharpness is heavily influenced by the step size and batch size Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2019; 2020), which cannot be
explained by existing optimization theory. Indeed, there are indications that the “Edge of Stability” intuition might
generalize somehow to SGD, just in a way that does not center around the (full-batch) sharpness. [...] In extending
these findings to SGD, the question arises of how to model “stability” of SGD.
Moreover, note that both the self-stabilization argument and our understanding of progressive sharpening are limited in
the mini-batch regime: (Damian et al., 2023) depends on having "one landscape with one curvature", and it is not easily
generalizable to the mini-batch setting; we do not know how progressive sharpening affects the higher moments of the
minibatch Hessian’s distribution. Importantly, our results imply that SGD is stable if on average the step is stable on the
mini-batch landscape—not on the full-batch landscape, see Section 8.

B. Implications: long version
B.1. Failure of Classical Optimization Arguments
Classical optimization theory comes with a stability constraints, e.g., for GD pick a step size η ≤2/λmax along the
trajectory, see Section C.1. These are required to prove a descent-type lemma telling the loss goes down at every step. EOS
result was so disruptive precisely because it makes the violation impossible to ignore: full-batch GD routinely operates with
curvature at (or beyond) the textbook stability boundary, the loss is not monotone, and yet training keeps making progress.
In fact, this is not merely “nonconvexity is hard”—it is a regime where the standard proof templates do not even get off the
ground for convex functions, and where trajectories need not converge to stationary points in the usual sense (Ahn et al.,
2022; Zhang et al., 2022).
One could hope that SGD was “safe”. Indeed, (1) along the mini-batch trajectories it was well known that λmax is smaller
(Keskar et al., 2016; Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2018); and (2) a clean, tight, stability threshold to use for mini-batch proof was
not previously known. In the mini-batch case, most analyses defensively assume a uniform smoothness bound over all
mini-batch objectives (see e.g. Gürbüzbalaban et al. (2021); Mishchenko et al. (2020)). But in practice that worst-case
Lipschitz constant is so large4 that it turns the question “is η too large?” into a non-answer: everything would be “too large,”
including the settings that train perfectly well. In this article we finally unify all these issues by characterizing the dynamics
and explaining a number of previous observations:
1. SGD is not safe either, descent-type lemmas are violated too.
2. We found a mechanism for which smaller batches force smaller Hessian (Keskar et al., 2016).
3. We have a much better, tighter, guess for what a too large step size is, one such that
Batch Sharpness ≥2/η.
Our empirical answer is simple and clean: SGD, as GD, “shouldn’t work, but does” in neural networks. Precisely, SGD has
its own edge, which is not set by λmax. What saturates at the 2/η threshold is Batch Sharpness—the expected directional
curvature of the mini-batch loss along its own stochastic gradient. Equivalently, the meaningful instability comparator for
SGD is the mini-batch landscape seen by the step, not the full-batch landscape seen by the theorist. In short, EOSS says
SGD breaks the monotone-descent intuition—but with the “edge” measured on the mini-batch geometry that SGD actually
experiences. Even better, this condition, unlike some proposed previously, is not a d × d × d × d object (Ma & Ying, 2021;
Mulayoff & Michaeli, 2024)—which is theoretically sharp but operationally opaque.
B.2. Failure of Arguments about Minima
Following claimes by Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1994; 1997) first, and Keskar et al. (2016); Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2018)
later, a large body of theory asked a location question: where does SGD end up, and why? There, intuition that the noise
enables escaping local minima and traveling towards flatter areas has been established in a number of settings—all of them
in the stable regime. In most of the implicit-bias literature, indeed, one assumes (or proves) that after some time the iterates
behave like descent on a fixed landscape and converge to stationary points, so it is mathematically consistent to attribute
“selection” to gradient noise on top of a deterministic drift (e.g., (Beneventano et al., 2024; Smith et al., 2021; Blanc et al.,
2019; HaoChen et al., 2020; Damian et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a; Beneventano, 2023; Shalova et al., 2024).).
EOSS forces an uncomfortable reframing. In the progressive-sharpening regime, not only SGD is not merely “GD + noise”
exploring a fixed landscape, but location of convergence needs to be changed to area of stabilization. Most importantly, the

mismatches in selected solutions (HaoChen et al., 2020)). Developing faithful continuous-time models for EOSS therefore
seems to require explicitly modeling batch-dependent curvature beyond mean-field or covariance-only descriptions.
C. Further work on the Framework
In this section we set up a minimal formal framework for how we reason about (in)stability in the rest of the paper. We
argue here that our goal is to isolate (i) instability criteria—possible sufficient certificates for divergence of a (stochastic)
dynamical system from a trusted region (multiple are possible here unlike in the deterministic case)—and (ii) practical ways
to recognize the regime where such criteria empirically saturate, which we call the Edge of (Stochastic) Stability (EO(S)S).
On the quadratic approximation of the loss, we show that the following three viewpoints are equivalent:
• breaking a valid instability criterion;
• observing a catapult, i.e., a large excursion outside the region where the quadratic approximation is trusted;
• observing a spike of appropriate size in the loss.
Importantly, thanks to this equivalence we know how to empirically diagnose EO(S)S and what we consider catapults in
Section 6.

C.2. Guiding Questions for EOSS
Quadratic and deterministic case.
To connect the discussion above with Cohen et al. (2021), consider a full-batch
method (deterministic) on a quadratic loss L. In this setting, the instability criteria obtained from Lyapunov functions of the
form in Equation (5) simplify, up to higher order terms in η, as follows:
(i) only full-batch quantities appear in the Taylor expansion, no higher cumulants of the Hessians {Hi}i;
(ii) shortly after reaching instability, the gradient aligns with the eigenvector corresponding to the largest eigenvalue λmax
of the Hessian (this is not the case for mini-batch optimizers, see Appendix K).
In particular, GD is an asymptotically (and Lyapunov) stable linear dynamical system if and only if λmax ≤2/η. Cohen et al.
(2021) empirically observed that GD trains neural networks in the regime where the inequality λmax ≤2/η is saturated, and
Damian et al. (2023) established this mathematically under an assumption of progressive sharpening, making precise that
saturation means λmax −2/η ∈[−cη| log(η)|, +cη| log(η)|].
Stochastic and non-quadratic case.
In the stochastic (mini-batch) setting, different valid Lyapunov functions (for
mini-batch or full-batch dynamics) typically give rise to different scalar quantities built from the Hessians—for example,
different moments or cumulants of {Hi}i—and there is no a priori reason for these quantities to coincide or to saturate at
the same time. For instance, the operator in (6) depends only on the first and second cumulants of {Hi}i, whereas Batch
Sharpness (even for a quadratic loss) also involves third-order terms, and λmax depends only on the first. This discrepancy
leads to our first guiding question in the box above—that having a valid instability criteria is not enough, as there could be
many such (not equivalent) criteria; we are looking for the one that actually saturates in the neural network setting, which
can only be established empirically6. Establishing if a given valid instability criterion creates an EOSS regime of instability
therefore requires this criterion to be “computable” in the high-dimensional setting of modern neural networks. The reason
why this might prove to be an issue is that some natural instability criteria cannot be efficiently estimated in high dimensions.

F.1. Two notions of oscillation
Definition 4.1 is still purely kinematic: it is agnostic to the underlying mechanism and only records the empirical fact that, on
the time window I, the loss repeatedly goes up and down and these upward and downward moves are interleaved with non–
negligible frequency. The definition is also independent of the long–term behavior of the dynamics: the running minimum or
running average of the loss could either go down, up, or stay roughly constant. In what follows we connect oscillations with
(in)stability by defining Type–1 (stable, noise–dominated wobbling) and Type–2 (unstable, curvature–driven) oscillations
and characterizing them.
When the trajectory exhibits oscillations (Definition 4.1) but a valid instability criterion on the local approximation is
saturated—or analogously when a perturbation of hyperparameters induces cataputs—we say the oscillations are of Type-2.
Unfortunately, Type-2 oscillations generally look like Type-1 when just inspecting the loss, thus the most reliable way to
understand if the oscillations are stable or not is through the method developed in Appendix C.3.

G. Proof of Proposition 4.4
G.1. GNI is not an Instability Criterion: Oscillations in SGD Are Expected
Mini-batch SGD with a fixed, linearly stable step size typically exhibits noisy oscillations around a minimum, even in flat
regions where the Hessian is small. This is classical in stochastic approximation (Robbins & Monro, 1951; Mandt et al.,
2016; Bottou et al., 2018; Mishchenko et al., 2020) and is precisely why step sizes are traditionally annealed like t−1. In the
language of Section C, such behavior is stable: the iterates wiggle but do not diverge, so oscillations alone cannot be taken
as evidence of instability.
Our framework of Section C makes explicit what instability is and what an instability criterion must achieve:
• Not simply: once its threshold is crossed, a single SGD step increases a quantity linked to a Lyapunov function of the
dynamics.
• But, importantly: this increase persists over many steps, forcing the trajectory into a catapult regime.
Thus a valid instability criterion for the local dynamics must rule out absorption into a stationary oscillatory state. GNI fails
precisely at this requirement. In convex settings with a fixed, linearly stable, step size η, SGD converges to a stationary
distribution π supported near a local minimum, even when initialized at that minimum (Robbins & Monro, 1951; Mandt
et al., 2016; Bottou et al., 2018; Mishchenko et al., 2020). In Proposition 4.4 and Appendix ?? we go further showing that,
if θ ∼π, then:
• GNI(θ) is centered at (or above) 2/η with large variance;
• The training loss time-averages at E[L(θt)] ≈η2/2.
• If one perturbs slightly η ↶˜η, GNI and the dynamics locally diverge to restabilize again to a new ˜π.
These are exactly the hallmark properties of stable Type–1 (noise-driven) oscillations.
Proposition 4.4 has two consequences:
1) The typical long-run behavior of SGD is to have GNI(θ) centered near 2/η—and to be in an oscillatory regime.
2) Observing values around this level or oscillations is not, by itself, a sign of being close to instability. Increasing slightly
the step size, increases the variance of the stationary distribution and the time-averaged loss; does not induce a catapult
or explosion.
GNI is therefore only a certificate of oscillation, not an instability criterion in the sense of Definition 3.1 and we expect
oscillations to happen even in the strongly convex setting.

J. Modeling of SGD: SGD vs Noisy GD vs SDE
In this appendix we will discuss the common ways to approximate SGD – with noisy GD and SDE.
We also present Figure 13 – a version of the Figure 6, but with the loss curves added to showcase the difference in the loss

J.1. Longer Discussion on SGD vs. Noisy Gradient Descent
If you are tempted to say “SGD is just GD plus some Gaussian noise,” here is the litmus test: inject noise into GD and see if
you recover the same curvature-limited regime. Under progressive sharpening, the answer is brutally simple: most noise
models miss the phenomenon.
To test this, we compare mini-batch SGD (batch size 16) against three noisy GD variants and an SDE integration (see
Appendix J for details): (i) GD with Gaussian resampling: Gaussian reweighting on samples (Wu et al., 2020), which
preserves the mini-batch structure (and injects noise into the Hessians). (ii) Diagonal noise injection: Gaussian noise
restricted to the diagonal of the SGD noise covariance (Zhu et al., 2019). (iii) Isotropic noise injection: Gaussian noise with
isotropic covariance (Zhu et al., 2019). (iv) SDE dynamics integration: as in (Li et al., 2017).
As shown in Figure 6 and Appendix J, only noise that preserves the higher moments of the mini-batch Hessians (and thus
the mini-batch landscape structure) yields an EOSS-like regime with λmax stabilizing well below 2/η. More generic noise
(diagonal or isotropic) fails to reproduce this behavior and instead behaves in a GD-like way. These experiments suggest
a structural separation: the stability threshold for mini-batch SGD is governed by Batch Sharpness, while noise-injected
GD/SDE proxies are governed by λmax. Notably, this is consistent with the message in Zhu et al. (2019) (though their focus
is generalization). Unsurprisingly, when the injected noise perturbs only gradients (and not the Hessians), the relevant edge
reverts to the GD one—λmax ≈2/η—as in (Ma & Ying, 2021; Mulayoff & Michaeli, 2024).
Challenges for SDE Modeling.
A standard response to “SGD is noisy” is to write down an SDE and move on. Our results
explain why this can be qualitatively misleading under progressive sharpening.

Standard SDE (and related) approximations cannot capture the location of stabilization of SGD
in neural networks under progressive sharpening, because they collapse mini-batch geometry to a mean-field picture.
Classical analyses typically ignore all Hessian statistics beyond the mean and work on a static landscape: (i) an online view
where each step uses an unbiased noisy gradient estimator, or (ii) an offline view treating SGD as noisy GD on the empirical
loss. In both perspectives, the full-batch Hessian is treated as the curvature object that matters.
Our results highlight a different mechanism: each update experiences a Hessian H(LB) that can differ substantially from
H(L), and it is the induced Batch Sharpness that stabilizes at 2/η even when λmax remains smaller. Prior work already
flags serious limitations of SDE-based approaches for implicit regularization: they can be mathematically ill-posed (Yaida,
2018), invalid except under restrictive conditions (Li et al., 2021), converge to qualitatively different minima (HaoChen
et al., 2020), or miss higher-order effects (Damian et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a). Recent discrete analyses (Smith et al., 2021;
Beneventano, 2023; Roberts, 2021) aim to address some of these gaps. Nonetheless, EOSS points to a deeper obstacle: when
batches are small, the geometry of H(LB) differs markedly from that of H(L), altering both eigenvalues and eigenvector
alignments—exactly the information that location and stabilization depend on.

Empirically, the criterion (38) behaves in a way that precludes it from being a

a way prescribed by a stabilization that’s based on higher order terms.
• Inconsistent level of stabilization. Finally, a notion that would govern SGD dynamics would present a consistent level
of stabilization, independent of hyperparameters. This does not happen in the case of second moment contraction.

N. On the fate of λmax
In this section we examine how λmax behaves once EOSS is reached and clarify its relationship to Batch Sharpness. A key
aspect of the original EOS analysis is, indeed, that the controlling quantity—the largest eigenvalue of the full-batch Hessian
λmax—has an immediate geometric interpretation. There exists an extensive literature about λmax size and role in neural
networks, and it is a main ingredient of any proof of convergence. The EOSS picture replaces λmax with Batch Sharpness, a
statistic whose connection to generalization and role in optimization theory is largely unexplored.

N.1. Empirical facts
Below the phenomena we extensively observe in vision classification tasks trained with MSE, ablating on batch sizes, step
sizes, architectures, datasets. See Figure 3 for a good reference of what generally goes on.
• Fact 1: Progressive Sharpening. λmax increases at most as long as Batch Sharpness increases.
• Fact 2: Phase Transition. Once Batch Sharpness plateaus at 2/η, λmax stops increasing. If it moves, it only decreases
from this time on.
• Fact 3: Path-dependence.
If changes to hyper parameters are made, Batch Sharpness changes abruptly or restart
growing and λmax also changes. Stabilization of both happen as Batch Sharpness reaches 2/η. The trajectory of λmax
is not fully determined by the size of hyper parameters (see Figure 11). That is, the level of λmax is path-dependent: it
inherits the history of progressive sharpening up to the moment EOSS is reached.
• Fact 4: Smaller batches ⇒flatter minima. Across every setting we tested, reducing the batch size monotonically
decreases the plateau level of λmax. This aligns with the long-standing empirical observation that smaller batches locate
flatter minima see, e.g., Keskar et al. (2016); Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2021)).
• Fact 5: A critical batch size marks the SGD →GD crossover. Each curve in Figure 36 exhibits a bend at b ≈bc(η):
for b < bc the plateau falls rapidly with b, while for b > bc it flattens and approaches the full-batch value. This bc
corresponds to the regime in which the mini-batch landscapes approximate well enough the full-batch landscape, restoring
GD-like dynamics (Appendix R.2).
• Fact 6: No universal power law. From static analysis, one would expect a scaling 2/η −λmax = O(b−α) for some α.
The log–log plots (bottom row of Figure 36) show no robust straight-line behaviour, ruling out such law for any possible
exponent −α.

N.2. Critical Batch Size
We can characterize two regimes for the stabilization levels (see Figure 37):
(i) Small-batch regime (b ≤bc): λmax stabilizes well below the full-batch threshold 2/η, signaling strong implicit
regularization by SGD. The stabilization level rises steeply with batch size, so even modest changes in b materially
affect the final curvature of the loss landscape of the solution
(ii) Large-batch regime (b ≥bc): the growth of λmax with b becomes much slower and the curve asymptotically
approaches 2/η from below, mirroring full-batch gradient descent and reflecting weak implicit regularization.
The critical batch size bc is therefore the point at which the training dynamics cross over into a full-batch-like regime. Works
as Zhang et al. (2024) study the following notion of critical batch size: "the point beyond which increasing batch size may
result in computational efficiency degradation". Likewise, works focusing on generalization performance depending on the
batch size (Masters & Luschi, 2018) identify a cut-off batch sizes above which test performance degrades significantly. We
conjecture there may be a relation between these quantities and leave a systematic investigation to future work.
N.3. Why 2/η −C/bα fails.
From linear stability analyses near the manifold of minima (Wu et al., 2018; Ma & Ying, 2021; Granziol et al., 2021;
Mulayoff & Michaeli, 2024) or random matrix theory (together with the fact that we have Batch Sharpness stabilize at 2/η)
one would expect to have a law of the form λmax ≈2/η −O(1/bα). Log-log plots of the gap 2/η −λmax in Figure 37b
shows no robust power law (for the lack of any linear dependency), invalidating this prediction (see also Figures 38-44).
Importantly, this does not invalidate the findings of those theories, instead showcases the insufficiency of a static analysis.
Indeed, those estimates are taken from changing the batch size statically, without making any training steps. In particular,
linear stability analisys does accommodate virtually any law, as long as there is change in alignment between the mini-batch
gradients. The fact that the static law does not apply means that there is a change to the alignment also happening. Therefore,
as will be discussed further in detail, the fact that these estimates do not apply means that to give faithful description of the
loss landscape at convergence one has to undertake an analysis that is path-dependent.

O. Other Quantities of SGD Dynamics
In this Appendix we explore other quantities that describe the SGD dynamics, and discuss their role from the point of view
of governing stability. In particular, we are covering the following quantities: trace of full-batch loss Hessian, λb
max (average
max eigenvalues of mini-batch Hessians), and a modified version of Batch Sharpness.
O.1. Trace of the Loss Hessian
A number of works Ma & Ying (2021); Wu & Su (2023); Agarwala & Pennington (2024) have linked the trace of the
full-batch loss Hessian to implicit regularization by SGD. We plot in Figure 31 and 32: λmax, Batch Sharpness, and the trace
of the Hessian along the training for a variety of models and batch sizes. We observe here that trace of the Hessian behaves
very similarly to the previously studied λmax. In particular, it does not have a consistent stabilization level, and depends
significantly on the batch size—with smaller batch sizes leading to lower stabilization level of the trace (aka flatter solutions).
Also analogous to λmax, it undergoes progressive sharpening, as long as Batch Sharpness is under 2/η. Analogously, the
stabilization of Batch Sharpness leads to stabilization of the trace. All of this showcases that trace of the Hessian is not the
quantity that governs stability of the SGD dynamics. Yet, it might be a useful indicator of the end phase of progressive
sharpening—in the potential situation when we have λmax stabilize, but other eigenvalues continue growing, as illustrated,
for example, in Cohen et al. (2024).
It is noteworthy that, in the context of MSE loss combined with piecewise-linear activation functions (e.g., ReLU), the trace

Q. Hardware & Compute Requirements
All experiments were executed on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU (80 GB) with 256 GB of host RAM. The software stack
comprises Python 3.12 and PyTorch 2.5.1 (built with the default CUDA tool-chain supplied by the wheel). The biggest
constraint is the VRAM when computing λmax, as it requires double-backprop of the computational graph of the NN. It
is possible to run the MLP experiments on MIGs with 10 GB of memory, other architectures require GPUs with higher
VRAM.
Baseline MLP (2M parameters, Section T)
Training for 100k steps on the 8 k-image CIFAR-10 subset finishes in ≈
5 min wall-clock while computing step sharpness every 8 steps, Batch Sharpness every 128 steps and λmax every 256
steps. Peak device memory is less than 1GB during ordinary training and ≈2 GB while estimating λmax on a 8k subset,
comfortably fitting a 10 GB VRAM GPU.
Algorithmic caveats.
We rely on power iteration for λmax; while Lanczos would reduce the number of Hessian–vector
products, the official PyTorch implementation remains CPU-only. In the later versions we switched to using LOBPCG,
adopted from the JAX implementation. For some speed-up, we cache the forward and the first backward pass through the
network to reuse when calculating the Hessian-vector products. We cache the eigenvectors from previous λmax calculations
to do either of the above algorithms with warm restarts, which provides significant speed-ups (e.g. reducing the number
of LOBPCG iterations from ∼20 to ∼1). This provides another reason for choice of power iteration or LOBPCG over
Lanczos, despite the one mentioned above—as most of the Lanczos implementations do not support warm restarts.
We also tried additional techniques to speed-up computations or reduce memory consumption. Batching vectors in the
Hessian-vector products (for use in LOBPCG) did not provide any noticeable speed-up. Using forward-mode AD did not
provide any noticeable reduction in memory consumption.

In this section we examine how λmax behaves once EOSS is reached and clarify its relationship to Batch Sharpness. A key
aspect of the original EOS analysis is, indeed, that the controlling quantity—the largest eigenvalue of the full-batch Hessian
λmax—has an immediate geometric interpretation. There exists an extensive literature about λmax size and role in neural
networks, and it is a main ingredient of any proof of convergence. The EOSS picture replaces λmax with Batch Sharpness, a
statistic whose connection to generalization and role in optimization theory is largely unexplored.

R.1. Empirical facts
Below the phenomena we extensively observe in vision classification tasks trained with MSE, ablating on batch sizes, step
sizes, architectures, datasets. See Figure 3 for a good reference of what generally goes on.
• Fact 1: Progressive Sharpening. λmax increases at most as long as Batch Sharpness increases.
• Fact 2: Phase Transition. Once Batch Sharpness plateaus at 2/η, λmax stops increasing. If it moves, it only decreases
from this time on.
• Fact 3: Path-dependence.
If changes to hyper parameters are made, Batch Sharpness changes abruptly or restart
growing and λmax also changes. Stabilization of both happen as Batch Sharpness reaches 2/η. The trajectory of λmax
is not fully determined by the size of hyper parameters (see Figure 11). That is, the level of λmax is path-dependent: it
inherits the history of progressive sharpening up to the moment EOSS is reached.
• Fact 4: Smaller batches ⇒flatter minima. Across every setting we tested, reducing the batch size monotonically
decreases the plateau level of λmax. This aligns with the long-standing empirical observation that smaller batches locate
flatter minima see, e.g., Keskar et al. (2016); Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2021)).
• Fact 5: A critical batch size marks the SGD →GD crossover. Each curve in Figure 36 exhibits a bend at b ≈bc(η):
for b < bc the plateau falls rapidly with b, while for b > bc it flattens and approaches the full-batch value. This bc
corresponds to the regime in which the mini-batch landscapes approximate well enough the full-batch landscape, restoring
GD-like dynamics (Appendix R.2).
• Fact 6: No universal power law. From static analysis, one would expect a scaling 2/η −λmax = O(b−α) for some α.
The log–log plots (bottom row of Figure 36) show no robust straight-line behaviour, ruling out such law for any possible
exponent −α.
R.2. Critical Batch Size
We can characterize two regimes for the stabilization levels (see Figure 37):
(i) Small-batch regime (b ≤bc): λmax stabilizes well below the full-batch threshold 2/η, signaling strong implicit
regularization by SGD. The stabilization level rises steeply with batch size, so even modest changes in b materially
affect the final curvature of the loss landscape of the solution
(ii) Large-batch regime (b ≥bc): the growth of λmax with b becomes much slower and the curve asymptotically
approaches 2/η from below, mirroring full-batch gradient descent and reflecting weak implicit regularization.
The critical batch size bc is therefore the point at which the training dynamics cross over into a full-batch-like regime. Works
as Zhang et al. (2024) study the following notion of critical batch size: "the point beyond which increasing batch size may
result in computational efficiency degradation". Likewise, works focusing on generalization performance depending on the
batch size (Masters & Luschi, 2018) identify a cut-off batch sizes above which test performance degrades significantly. We
conjecture there may be a relation between these quantities and leave a systematic investigation to future work.
R.3. Why 2/η −C/bα fails.
From linear stability analyses near the manifold of minima (Wu et al., 2018; Ma & Ying, 2021; Granziol et al., 2021;
Mulayoff & Michaeli, 2024) or random matrix theory (together with the fact that we have Batch Sharpness stabilize at 2/η)
one would expect to have a law of the form λmax ≈2/η −O(1/bα). Log-log plots of the gap 2/η −λmax in Figure 37b
shows no robust power law (for the lack of any linear dependency), invalidating this prediction (see also Figures 38-44).
Importantly, this does not invalidate the findings of those theories, instead showcases the insufficiency of a static analysis.
Indeed, those estimates are taken from changing the batch size statically, without making any training steps. In particular,
linear stability analisys does accommodate virtually any law, as long as there is change in alignment between the mini-batch
gradients. The fact that the static law does not apply means that there is a change to the alignment also happening. Therefore,
as will be discussed further in detail, the fact that these estimates do not apply means that to give faithful description of the
loss landscape at convergence one has to undertake an analysis that is path-dependent.

R.5. Implications and Open Questions
The findings above lead to the following main conclusions.
(C1) λmax is not the stability limiter for mini-batch training. Batch Sharpness governs EOSS; λmax follows. λmax is
capped from above by the value it reaches at the phase transition characterized by Batch Sharpness reaching 2/η. This
and Facts 1—3 above imply that:
The stabilization of λmax is a by-product of EOSS, not the quantity that governs it.
(C2) A theory of λmax has to account for the correct progressive sharpening. By fixing the model and changing batch size
b, the gap between the maximal eigenvalue of E

λmax(H(LB))

and λmax = λmax(H) = λmaxE[H(LB)] scales as
1/b. Any theory that keeps the parameter vector fixed and only varies b, or anyways leads to a power law, misses the
path-dependent descent that determines where training arrives and where λmax stabilizes. Facts 3 and 6 thus imply that
analysis of λmax is insufficient if it does not account for (1) the precise and correct effect of progressive sharpening on
the higher moments of the Hessian and (2) the correct alignment between mini-batch steps and Hessians.
Quantifying the plateau of λmax is thus still an (important) open problem. A complete account will require a dynamical
theory through the progressive-sharpening phase and beyond. Not just properties at its endpoint as for full-batch methods.
